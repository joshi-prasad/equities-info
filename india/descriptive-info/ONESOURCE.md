# Business Model

Here is a first-principles, evidence-based breakdown of OneSource Specialty Pharma’s economic reality, stripping away the presentation gloss to identify true drivers of value and risk.

### a) What Does the Company Actually Do?
OneSource is a pure-play specialty pharmaceutical Contract development and manufacturing organization (CDMO).

The company does **not** own the brands or the end-market products it makes. Instead, it solves a critical supply chain problem for major generic and innovator pharmaceutical companies: the extreme global shortage of complex manufacturing capacity.

Pharmaceutical companies outsource to OneSource to handle the complex, capital-intensive, and highly regulated steps of physically making a drug. OneSource operates in the "fill-finish" and final assembly stages of the value chain. For example, they take a customer's active pharmaceutical ingredient (API), formulate it, fill it into sterile cartridges or syringes, and physically assemble the final auto-injector pen. They box it, serialize it, and ship the finished product.

Economically, the CDMO model works on fee-for-service and volume-based pricing. The company charges based on the manufacturing volume and the number of "sterile days" utilized in their facilities, meaning their revenue is completely agnostic to the end-market price of the drug (whether it is sold in Canada or India).

### b) Business Segments
OneSource operates across four main modalities:

*   **Drug Device Combinations (DDCs) / GLP-1s:** This is the core growth engine. OneSource fills sterile cartridges and assembles them into pens/auto-injectors (currently holding 11 different device platforms). They cater to generic Semaglutide and Liraglutide players. **Economics:** Highly attractive and scalable, offering premium margins. Customers reserve capacity via take-or-pay contracts and upfront fees.
*   **Biologics:** A nascent but rapidly growing segment offering drug substance and drug product manufacturing. **Economics:** Gestation periods are long, but tailwinds from the US BIOSECURE Act and simplified FDA biosimilar guidelines have expanded their RFP funnel 4x YoY. This is development-oriented currently, transitioning to commercialization later.
*   **Sterile Injectables:** The legacy base business involving vials and pre-filled syringes. **Economics:** A steadier, cash-generating business but inherently competitive and subject to seasonality (e.g., antibiotics demand).
*   **Softgel / Oral Technologies:** Specialized oral capsules. **Economics:** Capacity has recently been expanded and is nearing full utilization. This is a steady manufacturing-oriented business.

**Growth Driver:** Management expects the DDC/GLP-1 segment to be the primary driver of their $400 million FY28 revenue target, followed by a longer-term contribution from biologics.

### c) Who Are the Customers?
OneSource's customers are a mix of global innovator companies, biotech firms, and top-tier generic pharmaceutical companies. Publicly named partners include Dr. Reddy's, Natco, Hikma, and Orbicular.

*   **Motivation:** Customers use OneSource because building sterile fill-finish and device assembly lines takes years and hundreds of millions in capital.
*   **Dependency:** Demand is heavily **regulatory-event driven**. If a customer fails to get Health Canada or USFDA approval, OneSource cannot commence commercial shipments.
*   **Stickiness:** Switching costs are astronomical. Once a CDMO is written into a customer's regulatory filing (e.g., an ANDA), moving to another manufacturer requires new regulatory approvals. Therefore, relationships are extremely sticky.

### d) How the Business Actually Works
The economic lifecycle of a OneSource contract follows a distinct transition that dictates their revenue volatility:

1.  **MSA Phase (Master Service Agreement):** OneSource conducts development work, formulation, and runs clinical/exhibit batches for the customer's regulatory filings. Volumes are low, taking up more line time (e.g., 2 batches in 7 days).
2.  **Capacity Reservation:** Customers pay upfront reservation fees or sign take-or-pay contracts to guarantee capacity for when their drug is approved.
3.  **The Regulatory Gap (The "Lull"):** OneSource builds inventory and waits for the customer to receive approval (e.g., waiting for Health Canada). Revenue recognition is deferred during this period.
4.  **CSA Phase (Commercial Supply Agreement):** Upon approval, OneSource shifts to commercial manufacturing. Output scales massively (e.g., 6 batches in 7 days).

**Why revenues are lumpy:** Revenue recognition often requires physical shipment or commercial launch. If an approval is delayed, the MSA ends, but the CSA cannot begin, resulting in sudden revenue air-pockets (as seen in Q3 FY26).

### e) How Is the Product Actually Manufactured?
The core constraint and value driver of OneSource is **sterile manufacturing capacity**, measured in "sterile days".

*   **Process:** The client provides the API. OneSource operates highly regulated clean rooms where the drug is formulated and filled into cartridges or syringes without contamination. They then assemble the mechanical pen device around the cartridge.
*   **Bottlenecks:** The ultimate constraints are line availability and batch size.
*   **Scale-up:** Operating leverage is unlocked by increasing the batch size (e.g., moving from a 200-liter batch to a 500-liter batch). This yields 2.5x the product output per day using the exact same number of sterile days, massively improving margins. This, however, requires joint regulatory approval with the customer.

### f) How Does the Company Actually Make Money?
Revenue is fundamentally driven by:
*   **Volume of units filled and assembled** (commercial phase).
*   **Development and service fees** (MSA phase).
*   **Take-or-pay penalties / reservation fees**.

**What increases revenue:** Shifting from MSA to CSA, increasing batch sizes, bringing new capex lines online, and customers entering new geographic markets.

**Why DDCs are attractive:** DDCs require specialized robotic assembly and specialized handling that commands premium per-unit realization. Once a line transitions from development runs to continuous commercial runs, the fixed costs are absorbed, and incremental margins expand sharply.

### g) What Are the Key Costs?
*   **Fixed Costs:** Depreciation on heavy capex (Depreciation jumped from 95m in FY24 to 279m in FY26). Facility maintenance, regulatory compliance, and debt interest (Interest cost: 89m in FY24 to 122m in FY26).
*   **Semi-Fixed Costs:** Employee costs. Management added 300-400 FTEs in FY26 to prepare for launches, front-loading payroll expenses before revenues materialized.
*   **Variable Costs:** Raw materials. (Note: API inventory risk is generally borne by the customer, meaning OneSource does not suffer severe write-downs if a drug fails).

### h) What Drives Profitability Up or Down?
**Operating Leverage is the primary driver.** Because staff and facility costs are fixed, EBITDA swings violently based on line utilization.

*   **Downside (Q3 FY26):** Customers did not get Canadian approvals on time. OneSource stopped taking new MSA clients to save room for expected commercial orders. With no MSA revenues and delayed CSA revenues, EBITDA crashed to a 6% margin (from 28% the prior quarter). Management called this "negative operating leverage".
*   **Upside (Q4 FY26):** Approvals came through, shipments began, and the fixed costs were absorbed. EBITDA margin immediately rebounded to 21%, with absolute EBITDA growing 5x sequentially.

### i) What Are the Key Business Risks?
1.  **Regulatory Dependency:** The company is utterly hostage to health regulators (FDA, Health Canada). The Q3 FY26 margin collapse proved that OneSource's financial health is directly tied to its customers' regulatory success.
2.  **Working Capital Stress (Critical Flag):** The narrative points to booming demand, but the balance sheet shows severe strain.
    *   Receivables exploded from 55m in FY24 to 690m in FY26.
    *   Cash Conversion Cycle worsened from 180 days to 230 days.
    *   Free Cash Flow is deeply negative (-561m in FY26) due to heavy capex. The business is burning cash to scale.
3.  **Contractual Softness:** Management admitted to voluntarily pausing the enforcement of "take-or-pay" penalties for some customers whose approvals were delayed, prioritizing long-term relationships over short-term cash. While strategic, it weakens near-term downside protection.
4.  **Debt Escalation:** Borrowings tripled from 562m (FY24) to 1,504m (FY26) to fund the $100M capacity expansion. If commercial approvals stagger further, debt servicing will pressure the P&L.

### j) What Makes This Business Work (or Fail)?
**The Moat:** Extreme scarcity of sterile fill-finish and DDC assembly capacity globally. Their USFDA-approved track record and ability to handle 11 different device platforms makes them highly attractive to generic players racing to capture the Semaglutide market.

**Failure Points:** The business will structurally fail if:
*   Generic Semaglutide pricing collapses, making the high-cost DDC CDMO model unviable for their clients.
*   Customers fail to secure regulatory approvals across multiple markets, leaving OneSource's newly expanded 220-million unit capacity sitting idle.
*   The massive working capital build (receivables and inventory) does not convert to cash, triggering a liquidity crunch.

### k) End-to-End Economic Flow
1.  **Engagement:** Customer signs an MSA and pays capacity reservation fees.
2.  **Development:** OneSource runs small clinical batches; generates low-volume, high-margin development revenue.
3.  **The Waiting Game (Working Capital Build):** OneSource buys raw materials and builds inventory in anticipation of launch. Inventory spiked to 439m in FY26.
4.  **Approval & Launch:** Customer gets regulatory clearance (e.g., Canada/India).
5.  **Commercial Supply:** Lines run at maximum speed (CSA phase). Revenue is booked upon shipment.
6.  **Cash Realization (The Lag):** Because OneSource is offering favorable terms to secure long-term contracts, receivables pile up (690m in FY26). Accounting profits look strong, but actual Operating Cash Flow (CFO) was a mere 9m in FY26 against an operating profit of 294m.

### l) Operating Leverage (The Key to the Investment Thesis)
OneSource is a classic high-fixed-cost, high-operating-leverage business.
*   They added 300+ FTEs to staff new lines. Those salaries hit the P&L immediately.
*   When a line is waiting for a customer approval (Q3 FY26), it generates almost zero revenue, but costs remain. EBITDA margin plummets to 6%.
*   When a line runs continuous commercial batches (CSA), incremental revenues drop almost entirely to the bottom line. This is why management targets a long-term EBITDA margin of ~40%.

### m) Evolution Across Quarters (FY25 Q4 to FY26 Q4)

*   **FY25 Q4:** *Euphoria & Aggressive Targets.* Management reports massive top-line growth (740% YoY) and 32% OPM. SEMA patents expiring in 2026 are the golden goose. Total confidence in $400M FY28 target.
*   **FY26 Q1:** *Aggressive Expansion.* Management announces a related-party M&A deal (Steriscience) to acquire EU assets. They pull forward $100M capex plans by a year due to "insatiable" customer demand.
*   **FY26 Q2:** *Reality Check & Caution.* Management tone shifts defensively. They refuse to give near-term guidance, citing ambiguity around customer regulatory approvals (specifically Dr. Reddy's in Canada). They admit to stopping new MSA onboarding.
*   **FY26 Q3:** *The Air-Pocket.* The anticipated risk hits. Revenues are deferred due to delayed customer approvals. EBITDA collapses to 6%. Management reveals they are not enforcing take-or-pay contracts to maintain relationships. The narrative shifts from "immediate explosive growth" to "wait for FY27".
*   **FY26 Q4:** *The Turnaround & Pivot.* Approvals finally land (Dr. Reddy's and Orbicular approved in Canada). Revenues rebound 47% QoQ, margins recover to 21%. **Crucially, management cancels the Steriscience M&A deal** due to stakeholder pushback on valuation, pivoting strictly back to executing their organic $400M goal. Confidence is restored, but grounded in operational reality rather than hype.

### SUMMARY CONCLUSION FOR THE INVESTOR
The narrative of a "GLP-1 supercycle" is real but **lumpy**. The market fundamentally underestimated the regulatory friction of generic approvals, leading to the Q3 FY26 earnings crash.

However, Q4 FY26 indicates the structural earnings upcycle is genuinely beginning. The primary risk over the next 6-12 months is not demand, but **cash flow conversion**. If the ballooning receivables (690m) and working capital metrics do not normalize as commercial shipments scale, the company's aggressive debt-funded capex could pressure equity returns despite rising EBITDA. The operating leverage is massive, but the balance sheet requires intense monitoring.

# Value Chain

### PART 1: LINEAR VALUE CHAIN

Base Inputs / APIs → Device Components & Sterile Inputs → Product Development & Regulatory Work (MSA) → Sterile Manufacturing & Fill-Finish → DDC Assembly & Integration → Commercial Manufacturing (CSA) → Packaging & Serialization → Customer Commercial Launch → Revenue Recognition → Receivables Collection & Cash Flow

***

### PART 2: STAGE-WISE EXPLANATION

**a) Base Inputs / APIs**
*   **What physically happens:** OneSource receives the raw active pharmaceutical ingredient (API)—such as semaglutide—directly from its customers or approved vendors. They also procure specialized components like cartridges, syringes, and raw sterile materials.
*   **What economically happens:** OneSource does not take the core inventory risk on the expensive APIs. While the balance sheet shows a massive inventory build (jumping to INR 439m in FY26), management confirms this inventory is fully funded by customer advances or purchase orders.
*   **Value added:** Low. This is a procurement and holding phase.

**b) Device Components & Sterile Inputs**
*   **What physically happens:** OneSource procures the mechanical plastic and metal components required to build auto-injector pens (managing 11 different device platforms).
*   **What economically happens:** This is preparation for assembly.
*   **Value added:** Low to Medium. Managing a highly complex supply chain of specialized device parts prevents bottlenecks later.

**c) Product Development & Regulatory Work (MSA Phase)**
*   **What physically happens:** OneSource runs small, clinical, or "exhibit" batches to help the customer file for regulatory approval (e.g., Health Canada, USFDA).
*   **What economically happens:** The customer signs a Master Service Agreement (MSA). Under an MSA, manufacturing lines run slowly—producing perhaps 2 batches in 7 days. Revenues are lower and are essentially "fee-for-service" development revenues.
*   **Value added:** High. OneSource's sterile manufacturing process and device assembly get written directly into the customer's regulatory filing (ANDA). This embeds OneSource into the product, creating astronomical switching costs.

**d) Sterile Manufacturing & Fill-Finish**
*   **What physically happens:** In highly regulated clean rooms, the raw API is formulated into a liquid drug and physically filled into sterile cartridges or vials without contamination.
*   **What economically happens:** This is the core bottleneck of the company. Capacity is sold in "sterile days" rather than unit volumes. If a batch size is 200 liters, the output is limited. If the customer and OneSource secure regulatory approval to move to a 500-liter batch, output increases 2.5x per day using the exact same fixed assets.
*   **Value added:** Very High. Sterile capacity is globally scarce, giving OneSource the power to demand take-or-pay contracts and capacity reservation fees.

**e) DDC Assembly & Integration (Drug-Device Combinations)**
*   **What physically happens:** The sterile, liquid-filled cartridge is mechanically inserted and assembled into an auto-injector pen (the DDC).
*   **What economically happens:** The customer gets a fully finished product that a patient can use at home.
*   **Value added:** Premium. Very few CDMOs globally possess the automated robotic capabilities to assemble 11 different types of injector devices. This commands premium pricing.

**f) Commercial Manufacturing (CSA Phase)**
*   **What physically happens:** Once the customer receives regulatory approval, the product moves from development (MSA) to a Commercial Supply Agreement (CSA). The manufacturing lines switch to maximum speed, producing up to 6 batches in 7 days.
*   **What economically happens:** This is where **massive operating leverage** kicks in. Because the facility and staff costs are largely fixed, running the lines 3x faster drops the incremental revenue directly to the EBITDA line.
*   **Value added:** Highest (from an earnings perspective). This is the inflection point where narrative translates to actual cash generation.

**g) Packaging & Serialization**
*   **What physically happens:** The assembled pens are boxed, labeled, and serialized for compliance before being shipped out.
*   **What economically happens:** Standard manufacturing step.
*   **Value added:** Low, but necessary to offer a true "end-to-end" solution, preventing the customer from needing a third-party packager.

**h) Customer Commercial Launch**
*   **What physically happens:** The customer launches the generic drug in end-markets like Canada, Brazil, or India.
*   **What economically happens:** OneSource's revenue is completely agnostic to the end-market geography or retail pricing. They charge a fixed fee based on manufacturing volume, completely shielding them from generic price erosion in the pharmacy channel.

**i) Revenue Recognition, Receivables & Cash Flow**
*   **What physically happens:** Product is shipped, or capacity take-or-pay invoices are generated.
*   **What economically happens:** A severe lag exists between accounting profits and cash. To maintain long-term relationships, management has voluntarily paused enforcing take-or-pay penalties for customers who faced Canadian regulatory delays. Consequently, OneSource is recognizing revenue, but receivables have skyrocketed to INR 690m in FY26, severely straining the cash conversion cycle (now at 230 days).

***

### PART 3: VALUE CREATION MAP

*   **Where maximum value addition occurs:** The sterile Fill-Finish and DDC Assembly stages. Building these capabilities takes years and hundreds of millions in capital, forming a massive barrier to entry.
*   **Where pricing power exists:** In the scarcity of the asset. Customers are literally funding OneSource's capex expansions just to guarantee access to the sterile lines.
*   **Where switching costs emerge:** During the MSA (development) phase. Once OneSource is listed as the manufacturer on a customer's health regulator filing, switching to a new CDMO requires restarting the regulatory approval process.
*   **Where operating leverage becomes powerful:** The transition from MSA (development) to CSA (commercial). A line goes from running 2 batches a week to 6 batches a week on the same fixed cost base.

***

### PART 4: BOTTLENECKS & RISK POINTS

*   **The Fragile Stage (Regulatory Approvals):** The entire economic engine stalls if health regulators (e.g., Health Canada) delay approvals for OneSource's customers.
    *   **The Q3 FY26 Crash:** This specific risk materialized in Q3. Customers did not receive expected Canadian approvals. Because the lines were reserved for commercial runs, OneSource had halted new MSA business. With no MSAs and delayed CSAs, the lines sat idle, fixed costs chewed up profits, and EBITDA margins collapsed to 5.97%.
    *   **The Q4 FY26 Recovery:** Approvals finally landed for partners like Orbicular and Dr. Reddy's. CSA runs commenced, fixed costs were absorbed, and margins immediately rebounded to 21.46%.
*   **The Batch Size Bottleneck:** The older lines are capped at 200-liter batch sizes. Scaling to 500 liters (a 2.5x volume increase) requires joint regulatory filings with the customer. If this execution fails, capacity will hit an artificial ceiling.

***

### PART 5: KEY INVESTOR INSIGHTS

*   **What actually drives OneSource economics:** _The company is fundamentally driven by **Line Utilization** and the **MSA-to-CSA transition**._ Narrative about "the size of the GLP-1 market" is irrelevant if customers' specific drug applications are stuck in regulatory purgatory. The PRIMARY economic driver is: _sterile line throughput, commercial run continuity, and utilization absorption_.
*   **What determines operating leverage:** Throughput speed. Because OneSource front-loaded 300+ employees and heavy depreciation (INR 279m) to prepare for launches, profitability is highly leveraged to commercial shipping volumes.
*   **What matters more than management narrative:** Cash flow conversion. Management paints a picture of massive take-or-pay demand, but the reality is they waived these enforcement clauses to be "flexible" with delayed partners. This indicates that customer power is slightly stronger than advertised.
*   **What investors MUST track every quarter:**
    1.  **Debtor Days / Receivables:** Currently at an alarming 177 days and INR 690m. If this doesn't normalize, the business is growing accounting profits while bleeding cash.
    2.  **Canadian / Emerging Market Approvals:** Management expects a "flurry" of approvals starting in Q1 FY27 (April onwards). Verify if these actually materialize.
    3.  **Capex execution:** Two new lines are supposed to come online by the end of FY27 to support the larger 500L–1000L batch sizes. Any delay here caps FY28 revenue potential.

# Growth Triggers

**CORE QUESTION EVALUATION:**
*Can OneSource successfully transition from underutilized sterile infrastructure into continuously utilized commercial manufacturing capacity before balance sheet strain becomes problematic?*

**Answer:** OneSource is currently in the most fragile phase of its transition. The business has front-loaded massive fixed costs (capex, depreciation, and 300+ new FTEs) to prepare for generic Semaglutide commercialization. Because management paused new MSAs to reserve capacity, delays in customer regulatory approvals caused a severe earnings air-pocket in Q3 FY26, collapsing OPM to 5.97%. However, Q4 FY26 indicates the inflection point has arrived: commercial shipments commenced following Health Canada approvals, driving OPM back to 21.46%. The critical race over the next 6-12 months is whether the continuous throughput of Commercial Supply Agreements (CSAs) can generate enough actual operating cash flow to relieve the ballooning working capital strain (-561m Free Cash Flow in FY26).

***

### 1. CORE GROWTH DRIVERS

#### A) MSA → CSA Transition (Semaglutide Commercialization)
*   **What is the driver?** Moving customer contracts from low-volume Master Service Agreements (development/exhibit batches) to high-volume Commercial Supply Agreements.
*   **Economic Mechanism:** MSAs absorb sterile days with low throughput (e.g., small trial runs). Under a CSA, the line runs continuous commercial batches, massively increasing unit output over the same fixed-cost base. This causes incremental revenues to drop almost entirely to the EBITDA line, creating operating leverage.
*   **Evidence:** Management noted Q3 FY26 weakness was entirely due to the absence of MSAs and delayed CSAs. Q4 FY26 saw the first Semaglutide shipments to Canada, rebounding revenues.
*   **Probability:** **High.** Approvals are now physically in hand for Canada (Dr. Reddy’s, Orbicular) and the US.
*   **Time Horizon:** Near term (next 2-4 quarters).
*   **What Could Go Wrong:** Customers fail to secure subsequent emerging market approvals, or generic pricing plunges faster than expected, reducing end-market volume demand.

#### B) Capacity Build-Out (40m → ~220m Cartridges)
*   **What is the driver?** Expanding the physical Fill-Finish and DDC assembly capacity at the flagship site.
*   **Economic Mechanism:** Current legacy lines are capped at 40 million units. Accelerating the $100M capex plan allows OneSource to accept new MSA clients while servicing massive CSA volumes. It unlocks absolute EBITDA scale.
*   **Evidence:** Management pulled forward Phase 2 expansion by a year. ~$80M is already committed. A second line is undergoing qualification and will be commercially ready by Q2 FY27.
*   **Probability:** **High** (execution), **Medium** (utilization). The capacity will be built, but filling it depends entirely on the cadence of customer approvals.
*   **Time Horizon:** Near to Medium term (FY27 onwards).
*   **What Could Go Wrong:** Building capacity ahead of approvals risks severe margin dilution (depreciation jump) if regulatory bodies delay customer filings.

#### C) Batch Size Expansion (200L → 500L/1000L)
*   **What is the driver?** Securing joint regulatory approval with customers to increase the physical size of each liquid fill batch.
*   **Economic Mechanism:** A sterile line produces one batch per day. Upgrading a 200-liter batch to a 500-liter batch yields 2.5x the product output per day using the exact same facility, staff, and sterile time. This drives explosive margin expansion and lowers per-unit costs.
*   **Evidence:** Management explicitly stated they are working actively with partners to scale up batch sizes to make customers more cost-competitive.
*   **Probability:** **Medium.** Requires seamless regulatory coordination between the CDMO and the customer.
*   **Time Horizon:** Medium term (FY27-FY28).
*   **What Could Go Wrong:** Regulators may demand fresh stability data or reject the scale-up, capping line throughput at legacy 200L levels.

#### D) Biologics Pipeline Activation
*   **What is the driver?** Monetizing the 4KL mammalian and 1KL microbial drug substance capacities.
*   **Economic Mechanism:** Geopolitical tailwinds (BIOSECURE Act) and simplified FDA biosimilar pathways are driving innovators to India. Initial revenues will be high-margin MSA/tech-transfer fees, transitioning to commercial supply later.
*   **Evidence:** The RFP funnel expanded 4x YoY. Added a global US biosimilar major with a 5+ product pipeline and an animal health major.
*   **Probability:** **Medium.** The funnel is real, but CDMO biologics have famously long gestation periods.
*   **Time Horizon:** Medium to Long term (Commercial manufacturing expected FY29 onwards).
*   **What Could Go Wrong:** Tech transfers fail, or customers run out of funding for clinical trials before commercialization.

OneSource has severely front-loaded its cost structure.
*   **Fixed Costs:** Depreciation leaped from 95m (FY24) to 279m (FY26). Interest expenses remain elevated at 122m.

Management's guidance of ~40% steady-state EBITDA margin is structurally plausible *only* under high utilization of the new 220m capacities. ### 2) HIGH-LEVEL FINANCIAL TRANSITION ANALYSIS

**A) What phase is the business currently in?**
OneSource is caught in a highly capital-intensive **commercialization transition phase**. They have aggressively front-loaded capital expenditure and operational costs (hiring 300+ FTEs) to prepare for generic Semaglutide launches. Capital is severely running ahead of commercialization.

**B) How are the financials behaving?**
Financials are exhibiting extreme, event-driven volatility. In Q3 FY26, operating margins collapsed to 5.97% (from 28.34% in Q2) because capacity was held idle waiting for Canadian regulatory approvals that were delayed. By Q4 FY26, as Canadian approvals arrived and shipments commenced, margins violently rebounded to 21.46%. This confirms the business has enormous fixed costs that require continuous throughput to avoid severe margin bleed.

**C) Is the business becoming more economically productive?**
Accounting-wise, yes; Cash-wise, no. The Q4 margin rebound proves that the physical lines can generate immense operating leverage when running commercial batches. However, the economic productivity of the business is currently terrible. Capital is being consumed by ballooning working capital, meaning the transition to commercial throughput has not yet translated into real cash generation.
