# Business Model

### a) What Does Aequs Actually Do?

Aequs is a **vertically integrated contract manufacturer** and **build-to-print precision engineering** company. It does not own intellectual property (IP) or proprietary product designs. Instead, it provides end-to-end manufacturing solutions for global original equipment manufacturers (OEMs).

**The Core Economic Engine:**
Aequs solves a specific problem for global OEMs: supply chain fragmentation. Traditionally, an aerospace component travels thousands of kilometers between different specialized suppliers for machining, forging, surface treatment, and final assembly. Aequs has consolidated these processes into a single Special Economic Zone (SEZ) in Belagavi, reducing component travel distance from 5,000 km to under 500 meters.

Customers outsource to Aequs to:
*   De-risk supply chains from China and Europe (China+1, Europe+1 strategies).
*   Reduce turnaround times, logistics costs, and global carbon footprints.
*   Leverage India's lower manufacturing costs while maintaining strict global aerospace-grade compliance (e.g., AS9100, NADCAP).

---

### b) Business Segments

Aequs operates two distinct divisions. The technological core is similar (precision machining, molding), but the economic profiles, scaling velocity, and market dynamics differ significantly.

| Feature | Aerospace Segment | Consumer Segment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Products** | Engine components, landing gears, aerostructures. | Toys, cookware, consumer electronics (portable computer/tablet enclosures). |
| **Customer Type** | Highly consolidated global OEMs (Airbus, Boeing, Safran, Collins). | Global consumer brands (Hasbro, Spinmaster, Mattel, top tech brand). |
| **Growth Opportunity** | Steady compounder. Shifting to higher-value parts (engines, nacelles). | High-velocity growth engine. Mass production for electronics just commenced. |
| **Manufacturing Complexity** | Extremely high. Titanium/superalloy machining. Long qualification cycles. | Moderate to High. Precision aluminium/titanium machining and plastic injection molding. |
| **Profitability** | Mature and highly profitable. 24.30% EBITDA margin in 9M FY26. | Currently depressed due to front-loaded fixed costs, targeting 18-20% at steady scale. |
| **Status in Portfolio** | The cash-generative backbone (82% of Q3 FY26 revenue). | The momentum growth engine (targeting 10x-18x revenue growth). |

**Analyst Takeaway:** Management is aggressively prioritizing the Consumer Electronics segment as the near-term growth engine. Aerospace took 15 years to scale; consumer electronics scales exponentially faster once capacity is built.

---

### c) Who Are the Customers?

Aequs caters strictly to Tier-1 suppliers and OEMs in highly regulated or globally scaled industries.
*   **Aerospace:** Airbus (Aequs holds "Detailed Parts Partner" or D2P status, giving them priority bidding rights), Boeing, Safran, Collins Aerospace.
*   **Consumer:** Mattel, Hasbro, Spinmaster, Wonderchef, Tramontina, and a globally leading consumer electronics player.

**Customer Dynamics:**
*   **Extreme Concentration:** The top 5 customers accounted for 73.17% of FY25 revenue.
*   **High Stickiness/Switching Costs:** Aerospace qualification is notoriously difficult. Once a supplier undergoes the grueling First Article Inspection (FAI) and process validation, OEMs are highly reluctant to switch due to the time and cost involved.
*   **Tenure:** The top 3 customer groups have an average relationship tenure of 15 years.

---

### d) Customer Engagement & Manufacturing Lifecycle

The business is characterized by high barriers to entry and long lead times to revenue:
1.  **Contract/MSA Award:** Securing a long-term agreement or "Life of Program" contract.
2.  **Engineering & Capability Build:** Defining the process flow, identifying infrastructure, and building specific tooling.
3.  **Prototyping & Qualification:** Extensive testing and validation to meet niche safety/quality standards. *This delays revenue realization significantly*.
4.  **Commercial Production & Scale:** Once approved, volume scales up across the program's life.

*Contrast:* In Consumer Electronics, the client dictates capacity requirements upfront. Aequs builds the capacity (machines, manpower) to meet that projected demand, meaning capital is deployed before revenue flows.

---

### f) Manufacturing Footprint & Capacity

Aequs utilizes an **asset-light footprint** by operating inside ecosystems developed by a related party (Aequs SEZ Pvt. Ltd.). Aequs leases the buildings and amenities rather than putting them on its balance sheet, accelerating its ability to scale.

*   **Locations:** Belagavi (Aerospace, 400 acres), Hubballi (Consumer Durables), Koppal (Toys), plus strategic proximity facilities in Paris, Texas (US) and Cholet (France).
*   **Capacity Explosion:** Installed capacity jumped from 3.96 million hours (Q3 FY26 annualized) to 4.70 million hours by Q4 FY26.
*   **Growth Bottlenecks:** Growth is primarily constrained by raw material lead times (titanium can take 52 weeks, specialty steel up to 75 weeks) and customer qualification timelines, rather than a lack of demand.

---

### g) How Does Aequs Make Money?

Aequs generates revenue by charging for the conversion of raw materials into finished, high-precision components.
*   **Revenue Drivers:** Total Contract Value (TCV) execution, increasing the "share of wallet" with existing customers (e.g., adding landing gear components for Airbus), and adding new SKUs. As of FY26, the Aerospace order book is robust at $889 million, providing visibility through 2031.
*   **Value Creation & Pricing:** Customers pay premium prices because Aequs guarantees zero-defect manufacturing, strictly complies with AS9100/NADCAP standards, and collapses the supply chain by offering machining, forging, and surface treatment under one roof.

---

### h) Cost Structure

**Variable Costs:**
*   **Raw Materials:** Titanium, aerospace-grade aluminium, stainless steel, and plastics. Currently highly dependent on imports, though management is trying to localize sourcing to improve margins.

**Fixed Costs:**
*   **Employee Costs:** Substantial and growing. Headcount expanded from 2,968 in Q3 FY26 to 3,153 by Q4 FY26.
*   **Depreciation & Overhead:** Aequs recently installed massive capacity (434 CNC machines and 179 molding machines by Q4 FY26).

---

### i) What Drives Margins?

Margins are currently the most critical variable for a momentum investor.
*   **Structural Drivers:** Value-added processes (moving from simple structures to complex engine/landing gear parts). Vertical integration limits margin leakage to third-party sub-contractors.
*   **Temporary Drags (The Inflection Point):** Aequs' Q3 FY26 EBITDA margin was 12%, dropping to 9% in Q4 FY26. *Why?* Management explicitly stated that they front-loaded fixed costs (hired employees, commissioned CNC machines) to ready the Consumer Electronics segment for mass production.

**Analyst Challenge:** Management claims the consumer segment will eventually achieve 18-20% steady-state EBITDA margins. Investors must monitor whether the top-line scaling in FY27 absorbs these new fixed costs rapidly enough to validate this claim, or if the consumer business structurally dilutes the 24% aerospace margins.

---

### j) Working Capital & Cash Generation

This is a working-capital-intensive business.
*   **Inventory Cycle:** Procurement is a major cash sink. Due to global supply chain constraints, lead times for titanium and specialty steels range from 52 to 75 weeks. Aequs must purchase and hold significant inventory to guarantee delivery to OEMs.
*   **Cash Flow Trajectory:** The company has historically relied on external funding and promoter debt to cover capex and subsidiary losses. However, the recent IPO (raising ₹814 crore) allowed them to aggressively deleverage. Net debt-to-equity fell to 0.1x in 9M FY26.

**Momentum Read:** If the massive upfront capex for the Consumer segment translates to swift revenue realization in FY27, free cash flow generation should inflect positively.

---

### k) Competitive Advantages

*   **Durable:** The Single-SEZ fully integrated manufacturing ecosystem. It is incredibly difficult for a new entrant to replicate the capital, land, and approvals required to put machining, forging, and specialized surface treatments on one campus.
*   **Durable:** D2P status with Airbus and Tier-1 status with Boeing. The qualification barrier acts as a moat.
*   **Temporary:** First-mover advantage in Indian contract manufacturing for specific consumer electronics enclosures. Over time, competitors like Tata Electronics, Foxconn, or Dixon could replicate this.

---

### l) What Could Break the Business?

1.  **Execution Risk on Consumer Ramp-up (Critical):** The CARE Rating report specifically placed Aequs on "Rating Watch with Developing Implications", noting that while capex for the Advanced Technology Plant (consumer electronics) is complete, the ramp-up has been "gradual" and stabilization is a "key monitorable". If this stalls, the newly added fixed costs will bleed the bottom line.
2.  **Customer Concentration:** Any downturn in Airbus/Boeing production schedules directly impacts Aequs.
3.  **Raw Material Shocks:** Inability to secure titanium or steel due to geopolitical issues, or inability to pass through cost spikes.
4.  **Related Party Complexity:** Heavy operational reliance on Aequs SEZ Pvt Ltd (a promoter-group entity) for facility leases, electricity, and water.

---

### m) End-to-End Economic Value Chain

1. **Procurement (Cash Sink):** Securing titanium/steel with 52-75 week lead times.
2. **Machining (Value Add):** CNC machines mill raw blocks to extreme tolerances.
3. **Forging & Surface Treatment (Moat):** Specialized chemical coatings, anodizing, and testing done internally rather than shipped globally.
4. **Assembly & Dispatch (Revenue Recognition):** Final product delivered to OEM.
5. **Cash Collection:** Payment received based on Long-Term Agreements.

---

### n) Operating Leverage

Aequs is currently exhibiting **negative operating leverage** transitioning into **positive operating leverage**.
*   They added ~185 employees and expanded capacity from 3.96M to 4.70M hours between Q3 and Q4 FY26.
*   Consequently, Q4 revenue grew by 47% YoY (₹3,671 Mn), but EBITDA margins contracted to 9%.
*   **The Momentum Thesis:** The depreciation and employee costs for the new Consumer Electronics capacity are now baked into the P&L. As mass production ships to the tech OEM in FY27, every incremental dollar of revenue will carry high flow-through to EBIT, driving ROCE toward management's 20% target.

# Landing Gear Business

### 1. Control and Actuation
*   **Target Components:** Rudder pedals, bellcranks, control rods, brake pedal quadrants, and control cables.
*   **Evidence in Sources:** The documents do not explicitly list the specific mechanical linkages requested. However, Aequs is aggressively expanding its broader "Actuation Systems" portfolio (which scaled from 79 SKUs in Q3 FY26 to 244 SKUs in Q4 FY26).
*   **Actual Components Manufactured:** Within this segment, Aequs explicitly manufactures **actuator pistons, housings, manifolds, mounting feet, mounting flanges, and jack heads**. Management notes that the housing and manifold products are used in aircraft actuation systems to control flaps and ailerons, and are rigorously pressure-tested for leakages prior to dispatch.

### 2. Hydraulic Systems
*   **Target Components:** Brake metering valves, accumulator units, shuttle valves, selector valves, isolation valves, and anti-skid modules.
*   **Evidence in Sources:** The sources do not specify the production of discrete metering valves, shuttle valves, or anti-skid modules.
*   **Actual Components Manufactured:** Aequs manufactures the **main fitting assembly**, which management explicitly identifies as "an integral component within the hydraulic systems of the landing gear assembly of an aircraft". Additionally, the manifolds and actuator pistons mentioned above interface directly with hydraulic controls.

### 3. Wheel and Brake Assemblies
*   **Target Components:** Axles, drive keys, brake piston housings, self-adjusting pistons, return springs, and rotor-stator units.
*   **Evidence in Sources:** While Aequs does not explicitly name axles, springs, or carbon disc brakes, they are active in producing critical structural elements for wheels and brakes.
*   **Actual Components Manufactured:** Aequs manufactures the **torque tube assembly**, which operates specifically within the braking system of the landing gear. For the wheel structure itself, they manufacture the **rim** and **half wheel**. Notably, management highlighted in the Q3 FY26 conference call that they have achieved "100% in-country value addition" for aircraft wheels.

---

### Manufacturer Role and Strategic Positioning

Based on the evidence, here is how Aequs fits into the supply chain for these parts:

**Primary Manufacturer & Sub-Assembly Provider (Tier-1):**
Aequs operates as a **build-to-print contract manufacturer** and a **Tier-1 supplier** to global OEMs like Airbus, Boeing, Safran, and Collins Aerospace. They do not own the intellectual property or proprietary designs for these landing gear components. Instead, they act as an end-to-end converter—forging the raw titanium or specialty steel (via their SQuAD joint venture), machining the component, applying NADCAP-certified surface treatments, and performing minor sub-assembly (such as the main fitting assembly) before shipping the integrated part to the OEM.

**Aftermarket / Maintenance Services:**
Aequs is structurally **not an aftermarket or MRO-driven business** out of India. When questioned about the MRO aftermarket opportunity during the Q3 FY26 earnings call, management clearly stated: *"We don't do any one-time delivery parts at all. What we do everything is under long-term contract... Aftermarket? We don't really pitch that because everything we deliver is under contract to the OEMs or tier one"*. The only minor exception is their Cholet, France facility, which segregates a small portion of its operations for MRO and aftermarket supply.

**Momentum Investor Takeaway:**
While Aequs may not manufacture every granular linkage or valve you listed, they are successfully targeting the highest-value sub-assemblies within the landing gear and actuation ecosystems (e.g., torque tubes and main hydraulic fittings). Management has explicitly identified "Landing Gear" and "Engine Components" as their primary margin-accretive growth levers designed to move the aerospace segment up the value chain. The dramatic increase in Actuation SKUs (from 79 to 244) and Landing System SKUs (from 124 to 187) between Q3 and Q4 FY26 indicates strong recent execution in capturing this specific wallet share.

---

**Notes:**
- Aequs is moving up the value chain, not just increasing volumes.
  - Simple machining --> Complex machining --> Critical structural parts --> Hydraulic components --> Landing gear assemblies --> Engine components
  - Every step upward generally brings: higher engineering content, tighter tolerances, longer qualification, fewer competitors, higher switching costs, and higher margins
- **SKUs have increased** meaning: _increasing wallet share inside existing programs_

# Misc Information

### 1. Structural Drivers: Why are Airbus and Safran Increasing Outsourcing?
Outsourcing to Aequs is no longer just about labor arbitrage; it is being driven by severe capacity bottlenecks and global ESG mandates.
*   **The Backlog Squeeze:** Airbus and Boeing face an unprecedented backlog, with a combined demand for 42,430 new aircraft over the next two decades. OEMs are stretched to their limits and are forcefully outsourcing non-core manufacturing to tier-1 suppliers so they can focus strictly on final assembly and design.
*   **Carbon Footprint Mandates (The Catalyst):** Global aerospace OEMs have aggressive "high5+" targets to reduce CO2 emissions by 65% by 2030. Historically, an aerospace component travelled 5,000 kilometers between isolated suppliers for machining, forging, and surface treatment. Aequs collapses this supply chain, reducing component travel to under 500 meters within a single campus, drastically cutting the OEM's Scope 3 emissions and logistics costs.
*   **Supply Chain Resilience:** OEMs are aggressively pursuing a "China+1" and "Europe+1" de-risking strategy to protect against geopolitical shocks and supply chain fragmentation.

### 2. Market Penetration: What Percentage of Wallet Does Aequs Currently Have?
*   **Current Status:** Management explicitly confirmed during the Q3 FY26 earnings call that Aequs currently holds a *"very small percentage of the overall market share of the customer"*.
*   **Expectations Gap:** Despite supplying over 5,600 SKUs and holding the prestigious "Detailed Parts Partner" (D2P) status with Airbus, Aequs is barely scratching the surface of total aircraft content. Management refuses to quantify exact dollar-content per aircraft, but noted that the Airbus A320 and A321 families currently represent their largest revenue share.
*   **Momentum Read:** This extremely low wallet share is highly bullish. Aequs does not need Airbus to sell more planes to grow; it simply needs to win more sub-assemblies (like landing gears and engine components) on planes Airbus is already building.

### 3. Operating Leverage: How Much Unused Capacity Exists Today?
Aequs is sitting on massive unabsorbed capacity, setting the stage for aggressive operating leverage. As of Q4 FY26, annualized installed capacity reached 4.70 million hours across 434 CNC machines and 179 molding machines.
*   **Aerospace Capacity:** Running at **70% utilization** (as of FY26). This leaves a ~30% runway to absorb the current $889 million order book without triggering massive new capex cycles.
*   **Consumer Capacity:** Running at a deeply depressed **23% utilization** (as of FY26).
*   **The Catalyst:** The Consumer segment has 77% unused capacity. Every incremental dollar of consumer revenue generated over the next 4 quarters will flow heavily to the bottom line, as the fixed costs (depreciation, employee overhead) are already baked into the current depressed P&L.

### 4. Return Trajectory: Which Businesses Have the Highest Incremental ROCE?
*   **Current ROCE Winner (Aerospace):** The mature Aerospace segment generates a robust **20% Segment ROCE** (FY26) with ~24% EBITDA margins. It is the cash cow of the business.
*   **Highest *Incremental* ROCE Winner (Consumer):** The Consumer segment posted a deeply negative **-15% Segment ROCE** in FY26 due to upfront capex. However, because the capital has already been deployed, the *incremental* ROCE on new consumer orders will be astronomical. Management projects the Consumer segment will eventually achieve 18-20% ROCE at steady-state scale.

### 5. Capital Efficiency: Which Ecosystem Creates the Highest Returns?
*   **The Belagavi Aerospace Cluster:** This is Aequs’ highest-return ecosystem. It integrates machining, forging (via the SQuAD JV), and surface treatment (via the API JV). Because Aequs captures the margin at every step of the conversion process rather than leaking it to third-party subcontractors, this ecosystem consistently delivers 24%+ EBITDA margins and anchors the consolidated profitability of the group.

### 6. Competitive Advantage: Does the SEZ Itself Create an Economic Moat?
Yes. The Belagavi Special Economic Zone (SEZ) is a highly durable structural moat for two distinct reasons:
*   **High Replication Cost:** It took Aequs 15 years to build India's first fully integrated precision manufacturing aerospace SEZ. Competitors cannot easily replicate the land, environmental approvals, and heavy capital infrastructure required to co-locate titanium forging, CNC machining, and NADCAP-certified chemical surface treatments on one 400-acre campus.
*   **Asset-Light Capital Allocation:** Aequs *does not own* the SEZ real estate. A promoter-group entity (Aequs SEZ Pvt. Ltd.) owns and develops the infrastructure. Aequs merely leases the bespoke manufacturing sheds. This related-party structure, while requiring governance monitoring, allows Aequs to scale capacity rapidly without trapping shareholder equity in non-yielding real estate.

India handles the massive, multi-year "Life-of-Program" serial production runs for platforms like the Airbus A320 and Boeing 737 families.
*   **End-to-End Vertical Integration:** India is where Aequs collapses the supply chain to drive its ~24% aerospace margins. While France might secure the contract, India executes the heavy, capital-intensive processes:
    *   **Heavy Forging:** Through the SQuAD joint venture, India houses the 10,000-ton closed-die hydraulic press for titanium, Inconel, and steel forging.
    *   **Surface Treatment:** The API joint venture in India executes the NADCAP-certified chemical treatments (anodizing, passivation, CAD plating). Management noted that this Indian ecosystem is *"very suited to do the landing gears"*.
*   **Sub-Assemblies & Finished Goods:** India handles the final complex assemblies before direct shipment to global OEMs. Aequs has transitioned from supplying basic parts to achieving *"100% in-country value add for select parts like aircraft wheels"* in India.

### 6. The Ultimate Proof of Transfer: The Hosur Aero-Engine Ecosystem

The clearest evidence of this "Incubate in France → Scale in India" pipeline is Aequs' recent corporate announcements.

To absorb the complex engine and landing gear manufacturing currently done predominantly in France, Aequs signed a massive ₹1,900 crore MoU with the Government of Tamil Nadu. This capital is being deployed to build a 250-acre greenfield site in Hosur, strictly dedicated as a **"New Ecosystem for Aircraft Engines, Landing Gear & Systems Components"**. This signifies that the high-volume serial production of France's most complex IP is actively migrating to India over the next 3 to 5 years.

### Analyst Takeaway for a 6–12 Month Horizon

Understanding this division of labour provides a very clear lens for evaluating Aequs' capital allocation:
1.  **Capital Efficiency:** The business model is highly efficient. By refusing to add capacity in France, management ensures shareholder equity is not trapped in low-return, high-cost European factories.
2.  **Margin Migration:** The French assets secure qualifications for the highest-value parts on an aircraft (engines, landing gears). 3.  **Near-Term Reality:** Because the Hosur engine ecosystem is a greenfield project, the margin accretion from transferring French engine volumes to India will likely not hit the P&L until **FY28**. For FY27, investors must continue to rely on the Hubballi Consumer Electronics facility filling its idle capacity to drive immediate earnings revisions.

# Strategic Model

Based on a synthesis of the DRHP, earnings calls, investor presentations, and rating reports, Aequs is fundamentally a **capability acquisition and industrialization platform** that uses its vertically integrated Indian SEZs as the commercialization engine to scale volumes and extract high-margin cash flows.

Viewing Aequs merely as a "contract manufacturer" fundamentally misprices how it generates its Return on Capital Employed (ROCE). The company explicitly acquires "DNA" (IP, qualifications, engineering know-how) via targeted global M&A and joint ventures, incubates them, and then forcefully transfers them into its high-volume, low-cost Indian manufacturing clusters to drive operating leverage.

Here is the step-by-step breakdown of how this economic engine operates, and what it means for a 6–12 month momentum investor.

### 1. The "Capability Acquisition" Engine (Securing the DNA)

Aequs rarely builds complex, high-barrier capabilities from scratch organically. Instead, it systematically acquires them globally or secures them via deep-pocketed Joint Ventures (JVs). This bypasses the multi-year learning curve and grueling OEM qualification cycles.

**Evidence of Capability Acquisition via M&A:**
*   **France (SIRA Group, 2016):** Aequs did not organically develop its most complex aerospace capabilities. The DRHP explicitly states the French acquisition was for the *"acquisition of complex aerospace capabilities"*, specifically providing them with the machining, assembly, and testing know-how for highly critical engine and landing gear parts.
*   **USA (T&K Machine, 2015):** Acquired to gain a North American footprint and specialized aerospace machining capabilities, keeping Aequs proximate to Boeing and Spirit AeroSystems.

**Evidence of Capability Acquisition via Joint Ventures:**
Management explicitly stated in the Q3 FY26 earnings call: *"We have leveraged global partnerships to enhance these ecosystems through joint ventures to gain deep process capabilities"*.
*   **Forging Capability:** Secured via the SQuAD JV with France’s Aubert & Duval, giving Aequs access to India’s largest 10,000-ton hydraulic press for titanium and superalloys.
*   **Surface Treatment:** Secured via the API JV with Canada’s Magellan Aerospace, granting Aequs NADCAP-certified chemical processing.
*   **Consumer Cookware:** Secured via the ACPL JV with Brazil's Tramontina, instantly acquiring high-end houseware technical capabilities.
*   **Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs):** Recently secured via the Ajna JV with Accel India and Vagus Defence, acquiring IP sourcing and licensing for India's defence requirements.

### 2. The "Industrialization & Commercialization" Engine (Scaling the Volume)

Once a capability is acquired and trusted by OEMs, Aequs shifts the mass commercialization of that capability to its Indian ecosystems (Belagavi, Hubballi, Koppal). This is where the actual economic value is created.

*   **Collapsing the Supply Chain:** Aequs takes the acquired capabilities (machining, forging, surface treatment) and co-locates them inside a single Special Economic Zone (SEZ). This reduces component travel from 5,000 kilometers to under 500 meters, slashing working capital requirements, logistics costs, and OEM carbon footprints.
*   **The Ultimate Proof of Transfer (Hosur MoU):** The clearest evidence of this model is the recent ₹1,900 crore MoU with Tamil Nadu. Aequs is taking the aero-engine and landing gear capability it incubated in France and building a massive 250-acre greenfield ecosystem in Hosur to commercialize it at scale.
*   **Cross-Pollination of Capabilities:** Aequs took the 5-axis precision machining and anodizing capabilities it spent 15 years acquiring in the aerospace segment and "leveraged" them to commercialize an entirely new vertical: consumer electronics (laptop enclosures and smart devices).

### 3. The Economic Logic: Why This Model Generates Premium Returns

*   **Risk Compression for Customers:** By acquiring capabilities via established European/US players or reputed JVs, Airbus and Boeing trust Aequs. Aequs leverages this trust to win the contract, but uses the Indian SEZ to execute the volume.
*   **Margin Capture:** Because Aequs owns the entire value chain in India (forging → machining → surface treatment), it does not leak margin to sub-contractors. This allows the mature aerospace segment to generate structural EBITDA margins of 18% to 22%, and ROCE of ~20%.
*   **Capital Efficiency:** The French and US facilities are purely for capability retention and customer access. Management has confirmed they are not deploying growth capex in these high-cost geographies; 100% of the capacity expansion is happening in India.

### 4. Momentum Investor Playbook: What Matters Over the Next 6–12 Months?

For an investor looking at a 6–12 month horizon, you must identify which capabilities are currently in the **commercialization/scale-up phase** (driving immediate earnings), versus which are in the **acquisition/incubation phase** (long-term noise).

**What to Price In Now (The Near-Term Earnings Triggers):**
1.  **Consumer Electronics Scale-Up:** Aequs has built the capacity (218 CNC machines added) and commercialized the capability for a top global tech brand. However, capacity utilization in this segment is sitting at a depressed 23%. CARE Ratings explicitly warned that the ramp-up of this Advanced Technology Product (ATP) contract has been "gradual". *The Thesis:* If Aequs successfully commercializes this capability and pushes utilization upward over the next 2-4 quarters, segment EBITDA will hit breakeven by Q4 FY27, driving a massive consolidated margin recovery from the current 9% levels.
2.  **Aerospace Breadth Expansion:** The company added 433 new aerospace SKUs in Q4 FY26 alone, pushing the aerospace order book to $889 million. This utilizes existing capabilities and drives immediate flow-through to the bottom line.

**What to Ignore Now (The Long-Term Incubation Projects):**
1.  **Hosur Aero-Engines:** While the ₹1,900 crore MoU validates the transfer of French capabilities to India, management's own timeline shows "Aerospace - Hosur - Revenue kick off" does not occur until **FY29/FY30**.
2.  **UAV Manufacturing (Ajna JV):** This capability acquisition was just signed in Q4 FY26. It is entirely in the R&D and incubation phase and will not contribute to FY27 operating leverage.

**Analyst Bottom Line:**
Aequs is an industrialization platform. The investment thesis for the next 12 months relies entirely on whether their manufacturing engine can successfully commercialize the **Consumer Electronics (ATP)** capabilities they recently built in Hubballi. If they execute, the embedded operating leverage will aggressively re-rate earnings. If execution stalls, the fixed costs of these newly acquired capabilities will continue to act as a drag on consolidated cash flows.

3.  **Surface Finishing:** Ultrasonic cleaning, abrasive blasting, high-end anodizing, and laser etching before final assembly.

**Bull Case**
*   **Operational event:** The global consumer electronics OEM accelerates purchase orders to match Aequs' newly installed Hubballi capacity (218 CNC machines).
*   **Operational event:** Aerospace division successfully dispatches the 433 new SKUs added in Q4 FY26 without raw material bottlenecks.
*   **Critical assumption:** Consumer segment EBITDA mechanically crosses from negative to breakeven by Q4 FY27 purely via fixed-cost absorption.
*   **Critical assumption:** Mattel successfully absorbs the Koppal toy cluster capacity left idle by Hasbro's abrupt exit.
*   **Invalidation:** Any pushback in the global tech OEM's product launch timelines or failure to meet yield expectations.

**Base Case**
*   **Operational event:** Aerospace maintains its 20% segment ROCE and 24%+ margins, acting as the financial anchor.
*   **Operational event:** Consumer electronics commercial shipments continue to flow but struggle to fully absorb the heavy depreciation load of the ₹830+ Cr gross block.
*   **Critical assumption:** The "gradual ramp-up" cited by CARE Ratings accurately predicts the consumer trajectory.
*   **Critical assumption:** Working capital days remain stretched (151 NWC days) as Aequs is forced to hold elevated inventory to secure aerospace deliveries.
*   **Invalidation:** A severe macro slowdown impacting both Airbus/Boeing single-aisle production and consumer electronics demand simultaneously.

**Bear Case**
*   **Operational event:** Consumer capacity utilization fails to cross the ~35% threshold required to cover direct manufacturing costs.
*   **Operational event:** Management deploys the additional ₹500 Cr consumer capex in FY27 despite existing capacity remaining deeply underutilized.
*   **Critical assumption:** The company is forced to structurally reduce consumer pricing to win volume, permanently capping the segment's margin potential below the 18%–20% target.
*   **Critical assumption:** Cash conversion cycle extends further, forcing the company to pivot back to debt-funding to survive the cash burn.
*   **Invalidation:** Immediate positive operating leverage if the tech OEM aggressively ramps up off-take.

### Final Verdict

For the Bull Case to play out, Aequs must flawlessly execute the commercial scale-up of its Hubballi Consumer Electronics facility, driving capacity utilization from 23% to 50% over the next four quarters; this will absorb the massive depreciation burden and snap consolidated EBITDA margins forcefully back to the 13%–15% range. The single biggest risk that could push Aequs into the Bear Case is a stalled "gradual ramp-up" in this consumer segment, which would strand over ₹1,300 crore in capital expenditures, permanently dilute the pristine 20% ROCE generated by the aerospace business, and trap the company in a severe operating cash bleed.
